## Peterson's Core Frameworks Applied

### 1. Big Five Personality Expansion
Peterson's aspect-level trait analysis provides granular predictive power beyond standard Big Five models, enabling identification of specific vulnerability patterns (e.g., high compassion + low orderliness = credential sharing risk).

### 2. Meaning as Antidote to Nihilism
Organizations lacking transcendent purpose create meaning vacuums that insider threats exploit. Peterson's emphasis on meaningful burden voluntarily accepted informs security culture development.

### 3. Shadow Integration
Unacknowledged personality aspects create psychological pressure leading to sudden behavioral shifts. Peterson's shadow work concepts enable early intervention before repressed traits erupt destructively.

### 4. Hierarchy as Competence Distribution
Peterson views hierarchies as inevitable structures serving competence recognition functions. Hierarchy-competence misalignment generates grievances that motivate insider threats.

### 5. Individual Responsibility and Sovereignty
Peterson's emphasis on personal responsibility paired with respect for individual sovereignty shapes accountability frameworks that prevent both negligent security practices and authoritarian overreach.

### 6. Archetypal Narratives
Drawing from Jung, Peterson identifies fundamental story patterns (hero, shadow, trickster, etc.) that individuals unconsciously enact. Security professionals can recognize these patterns to predict behavioral trajectories.

## Limitations and Ethical Considerations

### Privacy and Discrimination
Personality assessment must respect individual privacy and avoid discriminatory applications. Use trait data for role optimization and support rather than exclusion or punishment.

### Probabilistic Not Deterministic
PERSONALITY_TRAIT patterns predict statistical tendencies, not individual certainties. High neuroticism does not guarantee insider threat; it identifies vulnerability requiring support.

### Cultural Sensitivity
Peterson's frameworks emerge from Western psychological tradition. Cross-cultural application requires adaptation to diverse meaning systems and hierarchy concepts.

### Professional Guidance Required
Shadow work and archetypal interventions require trained psychological professionals. Organizational implementation should involve licensed practitioners.
