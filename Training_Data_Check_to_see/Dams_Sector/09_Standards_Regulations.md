# Dams Sector - Standards & Regulations

**Document Version:** 1.0
**Classification:** Regulatory Compliance
**Last Updated:** 2025-11-05
**Scope:** Federal, State, and Industry Standards

## Executive Summary

Dam operations are governed by multiple regulatory frameworks spanning dam safety, environmental protection, grid interconnection, and cybersecurity. This document details applicable standards, regulations, and compliance requirements for the dams sector.

---

## 1. Dam Safety Regulations (50+ Patterns)

### 1.1 Federal Energy Regulatory Commission (FERC)

**FERC-001: Part 12 Dam Safety Program**
```
REGULATORY AUTHORITY:
- Federal Power Act: Sections 4, 10, 15
- Jurisdiction: Hydroelectric projects >5 MW, interstate commerce
- Exemptions: Federal dams (USACE, BOR), non-power dams <5MW

KEY REQUIREMENTS:
Dam Safety Surveillance and Monitoring (18 CFR §12.10):
- Continuous surveillance: Daily visual inspections
- Instrumentation monitoring: Automated real-time monitoring
- Independent consultants: Periodic comprehensive assessments
- Reporting: Annual reports, incident reports

Five-Year Independent Consultant Inspection (18 CFR §12.37):
- Frequency: Every 5 years minimum
- Scope: Comprehensive examination of all structures
- Inspector qualifications: PE license, dam experience
- Report submission: Within 90 days of inspection
- Contents: Condition assessment, recommendations, photos

PART 12D REQUIREMENTS (High Hazard Potential):
- Emergency Action Plans (EAP): Mandatory for all high-hazard dams
- Inundation maps: Dam breach analysis, downstream impacts
- Notification procedures: Warning systems, emergency contacts
- Exercises: EAP drills annually or biennially
- Updates: Review and revise every 5 years

INSTRUMENTATION REQUIREMENTS (Engineering Guidelines):
Concrete Dams:
- Piezometers: Uplift pressure monitoring
- Seepage weirs: Quantify foundation seepage
- Survey monuments: Horizontal and vertical movement
- Plumb lines: Vertical alignment
- Strong motion accelerometers: Seismic response (if seismic zone)

Embankment Dams:
- Piezometers: Phreatic surface, pore pressure
- Inclinometers: Horizontal deformation
- Settlement plates: Vertical settlement
- Seepage weirs: Toe drain flow measurement
- Slope indicators: Surface movement

COMPLIANCE ENFORCEMENT:
- Notices of Violation (NOV): Formal citation for non-compliance
- Civil penalties: Up to $10,000 per day per violation
- License revocation: For continued non-compliance
- Criminal penalties: Knowingly endangering human life
```

**FERC-002: Licensing and Relicensing**
```
ORIGINAL LICENSE:
- Duration: 30-50 years typical
- Process timeline: 5-7 years from pre-filing to issuance
- Studies required: Environmental (NEPA), engineering, recreation
- Stakeholder involvement: Federal/state agencies, tribes, NGOs, public
- License articles: Operational conditions, environmental measures

RELICENSING (Integrated Licensing Process - ILP):
Stage 1 - Pre-Filing (2-3 years):
- Notice of Intent (NOI)
- Pre-Application Document (PAD)
- Scoping meetings
- Study plan development and approval
- Conduct studies (1-2 field seasons)

Stage 2 - Filing (1 year):
- License application filed
- Final environmental document (EA or EIS)
- Section 10(j) fishway recommendations
- Section 18 fish passage prescriptions
- Water quality certification (Section 401)

Stage 3 - Post-Filing (1-2 years):
- FERC review and analysis
- Settlements and stipulations
- Hearing (if contested)
- License order issuance

ENVIRONMENTAL MEASURES:
- Minimum flows: Year-round or seasonal
- Ramping rates: Limits on flow changes (e.g., 1 ft/hr)
- Fish passage: Upstream and/or downstream facilities
- Water quality: Temperature, dissolved oxygen
- Recreation: Boat ramps, trails, flow releases
- Cultural resources: Protection of historic/tribal sites

COST IMPLICATIONS:
- Relicensing process: $5M-$50M depending on complexity
- Environmental studies: $1M-$10M
- Capital measures: $10M-$500M (fish passage, recreation)
- Annual O&M: $500K-$5M (environmental compliance)
```

### 1.2 U.S. Army Corps of Engineers (USACE)

**USACE-001: Dam Safety Program**
```
JURISDICTION:
- USACE-owned dams: ~700 dams nationwide
- Regulatory authority: Section 14 of Rivers and Harbors Act (permits)
- Non-federal dam inspections: National Dam Safety Program support

USACE DAM SAFETY PROGRAM:
Risk-Informed Decision Making:
- Potential Failure Mode Analysis (PFMA): Identify credible failure scenarios
- Risk assessment: Estimate probability and consequences
- Portfolio Risk Assessment (PRA): System-wide risk evaluation
- Risk reduction prioritization: Focus on highest-risk dams

Inspection Program:
- Routine inspections: Annual or more frequent
- Periodic inspections: Every 5 years (formal)
- Special inspections: Post-earthquake, extreme flood events
- Dam safety modification studies: For deficient dams

DAM SAFETY MODIFICATION PROGRAM (DSMP):
- Funding: Congressional appropriations
- Projects: Seismic upgrades, spillway capacity increases
- Examples: Folsom Dam ($900M), Herbert Hoover Dike ($4B)
- Timeline: Planning to completion 10-20 years

ENGINEERING STANDARDS:
- ER 1110-2-1806: Earthquake Design and Evaluation for Civil Works Projects
- EM 1110-2-2200: Gravity Dam Design
- EM 1110-2-2300: Earth and Rock-Fill Dam Design
- EM 1110-2-1603: Hydraulic Design of Spillways
```

**USACE-002: Section 408 Permits**
```
REGULATORY PROGRAM:
Authority: 33 USC 408
Requirement: Permission to alter USACE civil works projects
Examples: Hydroelectric retrofits at USACE dams, pipeline crossings

PERMIT PROCESS:
1. Pre-application meeting: Discuss scope, requirements
2. Formal application: Engineering plans, environmental analysis
3. USACE review: Technical analysis, NEPA compliance
4. Public involvement: Public notice, comment period (if significant)
5. Decision: Approval, conditional approval, or denial
6. Monitoring: Construction oversight, as-built verification

EVALUATION CRITERIA:
- Not injurious to public interest
- No adverse impact on structural integrity
- Impairment of usefulness: No reduction in project benefits
- Environmental compliance: NEPA, ESA, NHPA
- Construction quality: Engineering standards, inspection
```

### 1.3 State Dam Safety Programs

**STATE-001: State Dam Safety Regulations**
```
STATE AUTHORITY:
- Model State Dam Safety Program: FEMA guidelines
- Jurisdiction: Non-federal, non-FERC dams
- Variability: Requirements vary significantly by state

TYPICAL STATE REQUIREMENTS:
Dam Classification:
- Size: Height and storage volume
- Hazard potential: High, significant, low (based on downstream risk)
- Classification determines inspection frequency, design standards

Inspection Programs:
- High hazard: Annual inspections by state or owner
- Significant hazard: Biennial inspections
- Low hazard: 3-5 year inspections
- Independent consultant: Periodic (varies by state)

Emergency Action Plans:
- Required: High and significant hazard dams (most states)
- Contents: Inundation maps, notification flowcharts, procedures
- Exercises: Tabletop or full-scale drills
- Approval: State dam safety office

Permitting:
- New dams: Design approval, construction inspection
- Modifications: Alteration permits required
- Repairs: May require notification or approval

LEADING STATE PROGRAMS:
California Division of Safety of Dams (DSOD):
- Strong regulatory authority
- Annual inspections (high hazard)
- Seismic evaluation requirements
- Piezometer data review

Washington State Dam Safety:
- Risk-informed approach
- Emergency Action Plan requirements
- Inspection reports publicly available

Colorado Dam Safety:
- Prescriptive design standards
- EAP drills required
- Instrumentation requirements
```

## 2. Electrical & Grid Standards (40+ Patterns)

### 2.1 North American Electric Reliability Corporation (NERC)

**NERC-001: Critical Infrastructure Protection (CIP) Standards**
```
APPLICABILITY:
- Bulk Electric System (BES): >20 MVA generators (with exceptions)
- BES Cyber Systems: Cyber assets essential to BES reliability
- Medium impact: Most hydroelectric plants 20-1500 MW
- High impact: 1500 MW and above, or critical to transmission

CIP-002: BES Cyber System Categorization
- Identify BES Cyber Systems
- Categorize as High, Medium, or Low impact
- Document cyber assets and their functions
- Review annually or when systems change

CIP-003: Security Management Controls
- Cyber security policy: Senior management approval
- Leadership: Identify CIP Senior Manager and delegates
- Awareness: Annual cyber security training
- Access controls: Electronic and physical access management

CIP-005: Electronic Security Perimeters
- Network segmentation: Define Electronic Security Perimeters (ESP)
- Access control: Firewalls, IPS at ESP boundaries
- Remote access: Jump hosts, VPN, multi-factor authentication
- Monitoring: Intrusion detection, event logging

CIP-007: Systems Security Management
- Ports and services: Disable unnecessary services
- Patch management: Within 35 days or mitigating controls
- Malware prevention: Antivirus, application whitelisting
- Security events: Log and review
- Passwords: Complexity, change intervals (15-90 days)

CIP-010: Configuration Change Management
- Baseline configurations: Document system configs
- Change control: Authorize and document changes
- Vulnerability assessments: 15-month intervals
- Transient devices: USB control, scanning

CIP-011: Information Protection
- Data classification: Identify BES Cyber System Information (BCSI)
- Access controls: Restrict BCSI access
- Disposal: Secure data destruction
- Reuse: Sanitize media before reuse

COMPLIANCE:
- Audits: 3-year cycle (on-site or remote)
- Self-certifications: Annual submission
- Spot checks: Random compliance verification
- Violations: Penalty range $0-$1,000,000 per day per violation
- Mitigation: Corrective action plans for violations
```

**NERC-002: Reliability Standards (BAL, FAC, PRC)**
```
BAL-001: Real Power Balancing Control Performance
- Frequency control: Maintain 60 Hz ±0.036 Hz
- AGC: Automatic Generation Control participation
- CPS1/CPS2: Control Performance Standards

FAC-001/002: Facility Connection Requirements
- Interconnection studies: System impact, facility studies
- Requirements: Voltage support, frequency response
- Modeling data: Generator models for planning studies

PRC-019: Coordination of Voltage Regulator and Generator Controls
- Generator reactive capability: Voltage schedule coordination
- Excitation system: Coordination with transmission voltage control

PRC-024: Generator Frequency and Voltage Protective Relay Settings
- Ride-through: Stay online during disturbances
- Frequency range: 59.0-60.5 Hz (continuous operation)
- Voltage range: 0.9-1.1 per unit (continuous operation)
- Settings coordination: Balance equipment protection and grid reliability
```

### 2.2 IEEE Grid Interconnection Standards

**IEEE-001: IEEE 1547 Interconnection Standard**
```
STANDARD: IEEE 1547-2018
APPLICATION: Distributed energy resources (DER) ≤20 MW
SCOPE: Grid interconnection technical requirements

INTERCONNECTION REQUIREMENTS:
Voltage Regulation:
- Voltage range: 88-110% of nominal (continuous)
- Voltage flicker: Limited to avoid customer complaints
- Power factor: 0.95 leading to 0.95 lagging (at point of common coupling)

Frequency:
- Normal range: 59.3-60.5 Hz (continuous operation)
- Ride-through: Mandatory for voltage/frequency deviations
- Anti-islanding: Detect and disconnect within 2 seconds

Synchronization:
- Voltage matching: ±10% of nominal
- Frequency matching: ±0.3 Hz
- Phase angle: ±20 degrees

ABNORMAL CONDITIONS:
- Undervoltage: Trip settings and time delays
- Overvoltage: Trip settings and time delays
- Underfrequency: Mandatory ride-through curve
- Overfrequency: Mandatory ride-through curve

INTERCONNECTION PROCESS:
- Application: Submit to utility
- Screens: Fast-track vs. detailed study
- Studies: System impact, facility design
- Agreement: Interconnection agreement
- Commissioning: Witness testing, approval
```

**IEEE-002: IEEE C37.106 Generator Protection**
```
STANDARD: IEEE C37.106-2021
APPLICATION: Guide for abnormal frequency protection for generators
SCOPE: Protective relaying best practices

GENERATOR PROTECTIVE FUNCTIONS:
Phase Fault Protection (87G):
- Differential: Compare generator terminals to neutral
- Sensitivity: 5-10% of generator rating
- CT ratio matching: Precise ratio correction

Ground Fault Protection (64, 59GN):
- Stator ground: 100% stator winding ground fault
- 95% ground: Secondary injection or third harmonic
- Sensitivity: 5-10 amperes typical

Loss of Excitation (40):
- Offset mho: Detect loss of field
- Settings: Based on generator reactance, system impedance
- Coordination: With under-excitation limiter (UEL)

Backup Protection:
- Voltage-controlled overcurrent (51V): Backup for faults
- Negative sequence (46): Unbalanced loading
- Overvoltage (59): Regulator failure
- Overfrequency (81O): Grid disturbance
```

## 3. Cybersecurity Standards (30+ Patterns)

### 3.1 NIST Cybersecurity Framework

**NIST-001: NIST SP 800-82 Rev. 3 - ICS Security**
```
STANDARD: NIST Special Publication 800-82 Revision 3 (2023)
APPLICATION: Industrial Control Systems (ICS) / OT security
SCOPE: SCADA, DCS, PLCs, and related cyber-physical systems

KEY RECOMMENDATIONS:
Network Segmentation:
- Separate OT from IT networks
- DMZ for data exchange (historians, reporting)
- Firewalls at zone boundaries (Level 3/2, Level 2/1)
- VLANs for segregation within zones

Access Control:
- Role-based access control (RBAC)
- Multi-factor authentication (MFA) for remote access
- Least privilege principle
- Account lifecycle management (creation, modification, deletion)

Monitoring & Logging:
- Centralized logging (SIEM integration)
- Log retention: 90 days minimum
- Security event monitoring (intrusion detection)
- Anomaly detection (baseline behavior)

Incident Response:
- Incident response plan specific to OT
- Playbooks for common scenarios (ransomware, unauthorized access)
- Communication plan (internal, external, media)
- Forensic capabilities (preserve evidence)

Supply Chain Security:
- Vendor risk assessments
- Contractual security requirements
- Software/firmware verification (hash checking, signing)
- Counterfeit component prevention

REFERENCE ARCHITECTURE:
Level 4: Enterprise / Business Network
- Firewall: Stateful inspection, application control
- DMZ: Historian replication, web HMI, remote access

Level 3: Operations / Site Management
- Firewall: Industrial protocol-aware (Modbus, DNP3)
- Services: Engineering workstations, asset management

Level 2: Supervisory Control
- Firewall: Unidirectional gateway or industrial firewall
- Services: SCADA master, HMI, local historians

Level 1: Basic Control (PLCs, RTUs)
- No firewall to Level 0 (direct connection)
- Protocol restrictions (whitelist IPs, function codes)

Level 0: Process (Field Devices)
- Sensors, actuators
- Serial or fieldbus communication
```

**NIST-002: NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) 2.0**
```
FRAMEWORK CORE FUNCTIONS:
Govern (NEW in CSF 2.0):
- Cybersecurity strategy aligned with business
- Roles and responsibilities defined
- Risk management program
- Cybersecurity supply chain oversight

Identify:
- Asset management (IT, OT, data, personnel)
- Business environment understanding
- Risk assessment (threats, vulnerabilities, impacts)
- Risk management strategy

Protect:
- Access control (identity, authentication, authorization)
- Awareness and training
- Data security (at rest, in transit)
- Protective technology (firewalls, IDS, antivirus)

Detect:
- Anomalies and events detection
- Continuous monitoring
- Detection processes tested and verified

Respond:
- Response planning
- Communications (internal, external, coordination)
- Analysis (investigate alerts, determine impact)
- Mitigation (contain, eradicate)
- Improvements (lessons learned)

Recover:
- Recovery planning
- Improvements (update plans based on lessons)
- Communications (restoration activities)

IMPLEMENTATION TIERS:
- Tier 1 (Partial): Ad-hoc, reactive
- Tier 2 (Risk-Informed): Risk management process developing
- Tier 3 (Repeatable): Formal policies, regular updates
- Tier 4 (Adaptive): Continuous improvement, lessons learned

PROFILES:
- Current Profile: "As-is" cybersecurity posture
- Target Profile: Desired cybersecurity state
- Gap Analysis: Identify improvements needed
- Action Plan: Prioritized initiatives to close gaps
```

### 3.2 IEC/ISA Standards

**IEC-001: IEC 62443 Industrial Security**
```
STANDARD: IEC 62443 series (ISA/IEC 62443 in North America)
APPLICATION: Industrial automation and control systems (IACS)
SCOPE: Security for OT environments across lifecycle

IEC 62443 STRUCTURE:
Part 1: General (Concepts, models, terminology)
- 62443-1-1: Terminology, concepts, models
- 62443-1-2: Master glossary
- 62443-1-3: System security compliance metrics
- 62443-1-4: IACS security lifecycle

Part 2: Policies and Procedures
- 62443-2-1: Security program requirements for asset owners
- 62443-2-2: IACS security program ratings
- 62443-2-3: Patch management
- 62443-2-4: Integration of new IACS components

Part 3: System Requirements
- 62443-3-1: Security technologies for IACS
- 62443-3-2: Security risk assessment
- 62443-3-3: System security requirements and security levels

Part 4: Component Requirements
- 62443-4-1: Product development requirements
- 62443-4-2: Component security requirements

SECURITY LEVELS (SL):
- SL 0: No security requirements
- SL 1: Protection against casual or coincidental violation
- SL 2: Protection against intentional violation using simple means (low resources, skills, motivation)
- SL 3: Protection against intentional violation using sophisticated means (moderate resources, IACS-specific skills, moderate motivation)
- SL 4: Protection against intentional violation using sophisticated means (extended resources, IACS-specific skills, high motivation)

TARGET SL FOR DAMS:
- High-hazard dams: SL 2-3 recommended
- Medium-hazard: SL 2
- Remote, low-hazard: SL 1-2

FOUNDATIONAL REQUIREMENTS (FR):
1. Identification and Authentication Control (IAC)
2. Use Control (UC)
3. System Integrity (SI)
4. Data Confidentiality (DC)
5. Restricted Data Flow (RDF)
6. Timely Response to Events (TRE)
7. Resource Availability (RA)

ZONES AND CONDUITS:
- Zone: Grouping of assets with similar security requirements
- Conduit: Communication path between zones
- Conduit protection: Firewall, encryption, monitoring
```

## 4. Environmental & Safety Standards (30+ Patterns)

### 4.1 Environmental Compliance

**ENV-001: Clean Water Act (CWA) Compliance**
```
REGULATORY AUTHORITY:
- EPA: U.S. Environmental Protection Agency
- State agencies: Delegated authority in most states
- Jurisdiction: Discharges to waters of the U.S.

SECTION 401 WATER QUALITY CERTIFICATION:
- Required for: FERC licenses, USACE permits
- Issued by: State environmental/water quality agency
- Contents: Water quality standards, monitoring requirements
- Conditions:
  * Temperature limits (e.g., <20°C at mixing zone boundary)
  * Dissolved oxygen (>6 mg/L typical)
  * pH range (6.5-8.5 typical)
  * Total dissolved gas (<110% saturation)
  * Flow requirements (minimum flows, ramping rates)

SECTION 402 NPDES PERMITS:
- Point source discharges: Powerhouse drains, cooling water
- Stormwater: Industrial stormwater permit
- Monitoring: pH, temperature, oil/grease, TSS (total suspended solids)
- Reporting: Discharge Monitoring Reports (DMRs) monthly or quarterly

SPILL PREVENTION CONTROL AND COUNTERMEASURES (SPCC):
- Applicability: >1,320 gallons total oil storage
- Plan requirements: Secondary containment, spill response procedures
- Training: Personnel spill response training
- Drills: Annual spill response drills
```

**ENV-002: Endangered Species Act (ESA)**
```
REGULATORY AUTHORITY:
- U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service (USFWS): Terrestrial, freshwater species
- National Marine Fisheries Service (NMFS): Anadromous fish, marine species

SECTION 7 CONSULTATION:
- Required for: Federal actions (FERC licenses, USACE permits)
- Biological Assessment (BA): Analyze project effects on listed species
- Biological Opinion (BO): Services' determination of jeopardy/no jeopardy
- Reasonable and Prudent Measures (RPMs): Minimize incidental take
- Incidental Take Statement (ITS): Authorized take level

TYPICAL DAM-RELATED ESA ISSUES:
Fish Passage:
- Upstream: Fish ladders, trap-and-haul, nature-like fishways
- Downstream: Screening, bypass systems, turbine shutdowns
- Effectiveness monitoring: Fish counts, telemetry studies

Water Quality:
- Temperature: Selective withdrawal, surface release
- Dissolved oxygen: Turbine venting, weirs, mechanical aerators
- Flow: Minimum flows, flow variability (ramping)

Habitat:
- Riparian: Flow releases for riparian vegetation
- Spawning: Gravel augmentation, flow timing
- Rearing: Side channel creation, flow refugia

NON-FEDERAL SECTION 10 PERMIT:
- Applicability: Private projects with federal nexus
- Habitat Conservation Plan (HCP): If take anticipated
- Incidental Take Permit: Authorizes take with conservation measures
```

**ENV-003: National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA)**
```
REQUIREMENTS:
- Environmental Assessment (EA): For projects with uncertain impacts
- Environmental Impact Statement (EIS): For major federal actions significantly affecting environment
- Finding of No Significant Impact (FONSI): EA conclusion if no EIS needed

EIS PROCESS (Typical for FERC Relicensing):
1. Notice of Intent (NOI): Initiate EIS process
2. Scoping: Identify issues, alternatives, analysis needed
3. Draft EIS: Analysis of environmental impacts
4. Public comment: 45-90 day comment period
5. Final EIS: Respond to comments, refine analysis
6. Record of Decision (ROD): Final agency decision

RESOURCE AREAS ANALYZED:
- Aquatic: Fish populations, habitat, passage, water quality
- Terrestrial: Wildlife, vegetation, threatened/endangered species
- Cultural: Historic properties, tribal resources
- Recreation: Boating, fishing, trails, access
- Socioeconomic: Demographics, economic impacts, environmental justice
- Cumulative impacts: Combined effects with other projects
```

### 4.2 Dam Safety Standards

**SAFETY-001: FEMA Dam Safety Guidelines**
```
FEDERAL GUIDELINES FOR DAM SAFETY:
- Purpose: Best practices for state programs
- Voluntary: Not regulatory but widely adopted
- Publications: Technical manuals, guidance documents

KEY PUBLICATIONS:
- Federal Guidelines for Dam Safety (FEMA P-93)
- Earthquake Analyses and Design of Dams (FEMA P-1071)
- Technical Manual: Conduits through Embankment Dams (FEMA P-1015)
- Selecting and Accommodating Inflow Design Floods (FEMA P-94)

HAZARD POTENTIAL CLASSIFICATION:
High Hazard:
- Loss of human life: Probable
- Economic losses: Excessive ($50M+)
- Lifeline disruption: Critical infrastructure destroyed
- Environmental impacts: Major, long-term

Significant Hazard:
- Loss of human life: Possible but unlikely
- Economic losses: Appreciable ($5M-$50M)
- Lifeline disruption: Disrupt service
- Environmental impacts: Appreciable, recoverable

Low Hazard:
- Loss of human life: None expected
- Economic losses: Minimal ($0-$5M)
- Lifeline disruption: Minor
- Environmental impacts: Minimal

RISK ANALYSIS:
- Semi-Quantitative Risk Assessment (SQRA): Estimate probabilities and consequences
- Potential Failure Mode Analysis (PFMA): Identify credible failure scenarios
- Risk prioritization: Focus resources on highest-risk issues
```
